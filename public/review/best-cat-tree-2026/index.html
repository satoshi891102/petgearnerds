Why Cat Trees Aren't Optional for Indoor Cats

Cat trees serve two critical functions often overlooked: they give indoor cats vertical territory (cats feel secure when elevated) and they redirect scratching from furniture to designated surfaces. For indoor cats, a cat tree isn't a luxury â€” it's an environmental necessity that prevents behavioral problems.

Cats that lack vertical space and scratching outlets develop: furniture destruction, inter-cat aggression (in multi-cat homes), anxiety behaviors, and general boredom-related issues. A $59 cat tree prevents hundreds of dollars in furniture damage.

Cat Tree Buying Guide

Height matters more than features. Cats want to be high. A tall tree with simple platforms beats a short tree with elaborate features. Aim for at least 4 feet tall.

Sisal rope quality is the durability signal. Thick, tightly-wound sisal lasts 6-12 months. Thin, loosely-wound sisal shreds in weeks. Run your thumb along the rope â€” tight winding means quality.

Base size determines stability. The base should be wider than the top platform. If the tree sways when you push the top, it'll sway when a cat jumps â€” and cats won't use an unstable tree.

Wall anchoring is mandatory for large cats. Any cat over 12 lbs needs the tree wall-anchored. No exceptions. A toppling cat tree can injure both the cat and anything in its path.
